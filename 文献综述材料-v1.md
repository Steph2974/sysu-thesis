      - ​      **Review of the Related Work**
      - 
      - 
      - ​    The body of research on Location-Based Social Networks (LBSNs) has grown significantly over the past decade, focusing on various aspects such as point-of-interest (POI) recommendation, user behavior analysis, and social relationship modeling. ([Sánchez et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2106.10069)) provide a comprehensive survey emphasizing the importance of LBSNs in recommender systems, highlighting the diverse information sources these networks utilize and the associated challenges faced by researchers in this domain [1]. One critical issue addressed in recent studies is the discrepancy between check-in data and actual user mobility. ([Wang et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.1609_icwsm.v10i1.14718)) investigate this bias and misrepresentation, revealing that check-in datasets often do not accurately reflect real user movements. Their crowdsourced study on Foursquare users underscores the motivations behind such misbehavior and the potential impact on research validity, raising concerns about data reliability in LBSN-based analyses [2]. In terms of recommendation models, ([Zhou et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.1016_j.eswa.2022.117147)) introduce a POI recommendation approach that considers the strength of user relationships, suggesting that social ties significantly influence user preferences and mobility patterns [3]. Complementing this, ([Al-Nafjan et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.3390_info13040188)) conduct a comparative analysis of matrix factorization algorithms—SVD, SVD++, and NMF—demonstrating their effectiveness in leveraging geosocial activity data for POI recommendations [4]. Temporal and spatial dynamics are also central to improving POI recommendations. ([Wang et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=ijcai-490-2022-07-01)) model the spatio-temporal neighborhood to capture the influence of user and location proximity over time, thereby enhancing personalization [5]. Similarly, ([Huang et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=sigir-3477495.3531801-2022-07-12)) propose a multi-relational framework, MEMO, which explicitly models heterogeneous relations and inter-temporal user-POI influences through advanced neural network architectures, addressing the limitations of previous simplified assumptions [6]. Graph-based approaches have gained prominence for their ability to model complex social and mobility patterns. ([Wei et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.1145_3554981)) develop a dual subgraph-based graph neural network (DSGNN) that captures social proximity and mobility similarity by constructing location and trajectory subgraphs, employing entropy-based random walks to mitigate data sparsity issues [7]. ([Wan et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.1080_17538947.2022.2122611)) further extend this by introducing iTourSPOT, a context-aware framework that leverages sequential contextual information to better infer user interest drift, thus improving next POI recommendation accuracy [8]. Knowledge graphs (KGs) are increasingly utilized to incorporate rich semantic information. ([Zhou et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=www-3543507.3583239-2023-04-29)) construct a hierarchical LBKG integrating diverse data types, including POIs, business areas, and their relationships, and develop a hierarchical learning model to extract both global and domain-specific knowledge, thereby enhancing socioeconomic indicator prediction [9]. Finally, trust and social influence are recognized as vital factors in POI recommendation. ([Acharya et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=doi.org_10.1007_s13278-023-01106-8)) propose a trust-aware spatial-temporal feature estimation model that incorporates social trust metrics to refine next POI predictions, emphasizing the importance of social relationships in understanding user behavior within LBSNs [10]. Overall, the literature reflects a multifaceted approach to understanding and leveraging the unique characteristics of LBSNs, addressing challenges related to data bias, social influence, temporal-spatial dynamics, and semantic richness to improve POI recommendation systems and social relationship modeling.

      - ​      **Review of the Related Work**
      - 
      - 
      - ​    The domain of next POI recommendation has seen a diverse array of approaches aimed at enhancing prediction accuracy and addressing inherent challenges such as data sparsity, geographical influence, and collaborative signals. Early efforts primarily focused on modeling sequential user check-in behaviors, often employing recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to capture temporal dependencies [2]. However, these methods tend to overlook the rich collaborative signals present across different users' trajectories, which are crucial for improving recommendation quality. To incorporate collaborative signals, Yang et al. introduced a user-agnostic global trajectory flow map combined with a Graph Enhanced Transformer (GETNext), effectively leveraging extensive collaborative information to mitigate cold start issues and enhance prediction accuracy [4]. Similarly, Li et al. proposed a Seq2Graph augmentation technique that propagates collaborative signals through correlated POIs across sequences, addressing the limitations posed by sparse POI-POI transitions [1]. Graph-based modeling has become prominent in capturing complex spatial and geographical influences. Wang et al. developed a Graph-based Spatial Dependency (GSD) module that explicitly models geographical influences via graph embeddings, thereby enriching the spatial context for next POI prediction [2]. Complementing this, Wang et al. introduced a disentangled representation approach within a graph framework, enabling the explicit modeling of different aspects influencing POI preferences, which leads to more precise recommendations [6]. Addressing data sparsity and resource constraints, Long et al. proposed a decentralized collaborative learning framework that allows users to train personalized models locally, enhancing robustness and privacy preservation [3]. In parallel, hierarchical multi-task learning strategies have been employed to reduce the search space and improve prediction efficiency. Lim et al. presented a Hierarchical Multi-Task Graph Recurrent Network (HMT-GRN) that learns user-region matrices at different spatial granularities, effectively alleviating sparsity issues and enabling hierarchical search for the next POI [5]. Recent advancements have also integrated hypergraph learning and disentangled representations to better model the complex spatio-temporal relationships and diverse influences on user preferences. Yan et al. proposed a Spatio-Temporal Hypergraph Convolutional Network (STHGCN) to capture intricate spatial-temporal dependencies [7], while Wang et al. introduced a Disentangled Representation-enhanced Attention Network (DRAN) that explicitly models different influence factors for POI representation [6]. Furthermore, the emergence of large language models (LLMs) has opened new avenues for next POI recommendation. Li et al. utilized pretrained LLMs to address limitations of traditional numerical models, offering a novel framework that leverages language understanding capabilities for location prediction [8]. Similarly, Yu et al. proposed the AST-PG model, which combines spatial grouping based on geographic and categorical features with temporal trajectory vectors, facilitating real-time and context-aware recommendations [9]. In summary, the landscape of next POI recommendation is characterized by a shift from purely sequential models to sophisticated graph-based, hierarchical, and language-empowered frameworks. These approaches collectively aim to better exploit collaborative signals, geographical influences, and complex spatio-temporal relationships, thereby advancing the accuracy and robustness of POI prediction systems [1], [2], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9].

      - ​      **Review of the Related Work**
      - 
      - 
      - ​    The field of sequential recommendation has garnered significant research interest due to its practical importance in modeling user behavior over time. Various approaches have been proposed to enhance the effectiveness and robustness of sequential recommendation systems. For instance, ([Xie et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=www-3442381.3449873-2021-06-25)) introduced the Adversarial and Contrastive Variational Autoencoder (ACVAE), which leverages adversarial training and contrastive learning to improve sequence generation and recommendation accuracy [1]. Addressing data sparsity and noise remains a persistent challenge in this domain. ([Liu et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2108.06479)) proposed a contrastive self-supervised learning framework, CoSeRec, to mitigate these issues by utilizing robust augmentation techniques, thereby enhancing model performance in complex scenarios [2]. Similarly, ([Li et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2111.10093)) developed RecGURU, an adversarial learning approach that constructs generalized user representations across multiple domains, even with minimal user overlap, leading to improved recommendation outcomes [3]. Temporal dynamics and evolving user-item interactions are critical factors often overlooked by traditional models. ([Fan et. al., 2021](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=cikm-3459637.3482242-2021-12-30)) proposed a continuous-time sequential recommendation model that integrates temporal collaborative signals with sequential patterns through a transformer-based framework, aiming to capture the latent temporal signals in user interactions [4]. Complementing this, ([Zhou et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=www-3485447.3512111-2022-04-29)) introduced FMLP-Rec, an all-MLP model equipped with learnable filters designed to effectively model dynamic user preferences from logged behavior data [5]. Furthermore, capturing heterogeneous and multi-behavioral interactions has been recognized as vital for comprehensive modeling. ([Yang et. al., 2022](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2207.05584)) addressed this by designing a Multi-Behavior Hypergraph-Enhanced Transformer (MBHT), which models various types of user behaviors and their dependencies, thus enriching the representation of user-item interactions [6]. Recent advancements have also explored the integration of language models into sequential recommendation. ([Li et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=kdd-3580305.3599519-2023-08-04)) proposed Recformer, which models user preferences and item features as language representations, facilitating better generalization to new items and datasets [7]. Building on this, ([Harte et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2309.09261)) examined the potential of large language models (LLMs) to revolutionize sequential recommendation by leveraging their powerful contextual understanding [8]. Efficiency and scalability are also addressed through novel neural architectures. ([Yue et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2310.02367)) introduced linear recurrent units (LRURec), inspired by recent language modeling techniques, to provide a more efficient alternative to self-attention-based models [9]. Additionally, ([Liu et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=cikm-3583780.3615134-2023-10-21)) proposed DiffuASR, a diffusion-based data augmentation method that generates high-quality synthetic interactions to alleviate data scarcity and noise issues [10]. In summary, the landscape of sequential recommendation research encompasses a variety of innovative techniques, including adversarial and contrastive learning, temporal modeling, multi-behavioral analysis, language representation integration, and data augmentation. These approaches collectively aim to address core challenges such as data sparsity, noise, temporal dynamics, and heterogeneity, advancing the development of more accurate and robust sequential recommendation systems.

      - ​    The concept of generative recommendation has gained significant attention as a promising next-generation paradigm in recommender systems, primarily driven by advancements in artificial intelligence generated content (AIGC) [1]. ([Wang et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2304.03516)) introduce GeneRec, a novel framework that leverages AI generators to produce personalized content guided by user instructions, marking a shift from traditional recommendation approaches towards more dynamic and user-centric content generation [1]. Large language models (LLMs) have emerged as a central component in this evolution, with research exploring their potential to transform recommendation systems. ([Ji et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2307.00457)) highlight the innovative use of LLMs based on textual data to facilitate generative recommendation, emphasizing the transformative potential of these models in revolutionizing recommendation methodologies [3]. Complementing this, ([Li et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2309.01157)) provide a comprehensive survey that discusses the progress, challenges, and future directions of LLM-based generative recommendation, noting that many existing works treat LLMs merely as feature extractors rather than fully exploiting their generative capabilities [4]. A critical aspect of implementing LLMs in recommendation systems involves how items are represented and indexed. ([Hua et. al., 2023](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2305.06569)) systematically examine item ID creation and indexing strategies for foundation models, illustrating the importance of effective item encoding in enabling LLMs to perform recommendation tasks efficiently [2]. Building on this, ([Tan et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=sigir-3626772.3657821-2024-07-14)) propose IDGenRec, which aligns LLMs with recommendation needs by representing items as semantically rich, platform-agnostic textual IDs, thereby enhancing the semantic understanding and recommendation quality [8]. Innovations in item tokenization further address the limitations of traditional identifiers. ([Wang et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2405.07314)) introduce LETTER, a learnable tokenizer that integrates hierarchical semantics, collaborative signals, and code assignment diversity, aiming to overcome the shortcomings of conventional ID, textual, and codebook-based identifiers [7]. Similarly, ([Chen et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2412.17171)) propose a self-improving item tokenization method that enables LLMs to refine their tokenizations during training, reducing inconsistencies and improving model performance [10]. Multimodal data integration represents another frontier in generative recommendation. ([Wei et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2403.10667)) advocate for a unified multi-modal personalization paradigm (UniMP), which leverages diverse data modalities while simplifying customization processes [5]. ([Liu et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2404.16555)) contribute with MMGRec, a multimodal generative recommendation model based on transformers, designed to incorporate multiple data types despite challenges such as inference costs and interaction modeling complexities [6]. Addressing the limitations of existing methods, ([Wang et. al., 2024](https://www.paperdigest.org/paper/?paper_id=arxiv-2405.07314)) propose EAGER, a framework that combines behavioral and semantic information to enhance recommendation effectiveness by exploiting the complementary nature of these data aspects [9]. This approach underscores the importance of integrating diverse information sources to improve the robustness and accuracy of generative recommendations. In summary, current research in generative recommendation emphasizes the integration of advanced LLMs, innovative item representation techniques, and multimodal data fusion to create more personalized, semantically rich, and effective recommendation systems. These developments collectively point towards a future where generative models play a central role in delivering highly tailored content, guided by user instructions and multi-source data integration [1][3][4][5][6][7][8][9][10].

      - 
      - 
      - ​    The concept of semantic ID recommendation has garnered significant attention in recent research, emphasizing the importance of representing items through meaningful, content-rich identifiers to enhance recommendation accuracy and model generalization. Wang et al. [1] introduce GNPR-SID, a generative model leveraging semantic IDs (SIDs) that incorporate user interaction patterns and POI semantic features, thereby improving semantic understanding and recommendation performance. This approach underscores the role of semantic IDs in capturing nuanced item semantics within a generative framework. Addressing the limitations of traditional semantic ID representations, Hou et al. [2] propose RPG, a lightweight framework designed to generate long semantic IDs in parallel, overcoming the typical restriction of short token lengths that limit expressiveness. This work highlights efforts to enhance the richness and descriptive power of semantic IDs, facilitating more comprehensive item representations. Further exploring the embedding strategies, Hu et al. [3] present AlphaFuse, which learns ID embeddings within the null space of language embeddings to mitigate issues such as semantic space degradation and underutilization of language information. This method aims to better align ID embeddings with semantic features derived from language models, addressing key challenges in semantic ID learning. In the context of joint search and recommendation tasks, Penha et al. [4] investigate strategies for constructing semantic IDs that perform effectively across multiple tasks. Their comparative analysis emphasizes task-specific and cross-task approaches, suggesting that the design of semantic IDs should consider the dual objectives of search and recommendation to optimize performance. The limitations of autoregressive models in semantic ID generation are tackled by Shi et al. [5], who introduce LLaDA-Rec, a discrete diffusion framework enabling parallel generation of semantic IDs. This approach addresses issues such as unidirectional constraints and error propagation inherent in traditional models, promoting more efficient and globally coherent semantic representations. Ju et al. [6] contribute by developing GRID, an open-source, modular framework tailored for generative recommendation with semantic IDs. This platform facilitates systematic benchmarking and rapid iteration, fostering broader adoption and experimentation in semantic ID-based recommendation systems. Expanding beyond traditional textual representations, Kim et al. [7] propose FusID, a modality-fused semantic ID framework for music recommendation. FusID integrates multimodal information through fusion techniques, reduces redundancy, and employs product quantization to convert continuous embeddings into discrete tokens, thereby capturing inter-modal interactions and improving item representation efficiency. Qiao et al. [8] revisit semantic ID learning by treating text as a visual signal, emphasizing the importance of side information in mapping items to discrete identifiers. Their empirical study suggests that leveraging visual signals alongside textual data can enrich semantic representations, enhancing the robustness of semantic IDs in generative models. In addressing communication paradigms, Han et al. [9] propose a feature-indexed approach that transmits feature code embeddings as codebooks rather than raw item embeddings. This shift aims to improve efficiency and scalability in recommendation systems by focusing on feature-level representations. Finally, Fu et al. [10] introduce DIGER, a differentiable semantic ID framework that enables end-to-end learning of semantic IDs within generative recommendation models. This approach emphasizes the importance of differentiability in optimizing semantic ID representations, leading to more effective and adaptable recommendation systems. Overall, these studies collectively highlight diverse strategies for semantic ID recommendation, including content-rich representation, parallel generation, multimodal fusion, and differentiable learning, reflecting a vibrant research landscape focused on enhancing the semantic understanding and efficiency of recommendation models.

        - ​     The literature on geographical modeling in POI recommendation demonstrates a diverse array of approaches aimed at capturing the complex spatial dependencies influencing user check-in behaviors. Recent studies emphasize the importance of explicitly modeling geographical influences through graph-based methods. Wang et al. [1] introduce a Graph-based Spatial Dependency (GSD) module that leverages graph embedding to explicitly model intricate geographical effects, moving beyond traditional sequence modeling techniques like RNNs. Similarly, Wang et al. [4] propose an Adaptive Graph Representation-enhanced Attention Network (AGRAN), which utilizes graph structure learning to replace static graphs with adaptive, similarity-based POI graphs, thereby capturing underlying geographical dependencies more effectively. In addition to static graph structures, some research explores dynamic and high-order geographical relationships. Li et al. [7] address the limitations of conventional graph structures that primarily focus on adjacent nodes by proposing a Region-aware dynamic Hypergraph with Dual-level Interaction Modeling (ReHDM). This approach captures high-order associations among POIs, reflecting the complex mobility patterns that extend beyond immediate neighbors. Furthermore, Pan et al. [6] introduce GeoCo, a geographical correlation enhanced network that employs a pre-training technique based on sentence similarity assessment to understand the semantics of geographical correlations, emphasizing the importance of semantic understanding in geographical modeling. Hierarchical and context-aware models also play a significant role in capturing geographical influences. Liu et al. [2] develop a Context Geographical-Temporal-Social Hierarchical Attention Network (CGTS-HAN), which extracts contextual information from user trajectories, integrating geographical, temporal, and social factors into a hierarchical attention framework. This model aims to comprehensively consider various contextual influences, including geographic information, to improve next POI prediction. Furthermore, some studies focus on disentangling geographical effects from user interests to address data sparsity and improve recommendation accuracy. Qin et al. [5] propose a geo-constrained negative sampling strategy that decouples geographical factors from user preferences, facilitating more reliable modeling of geographical influences. Similarly, Zhou et al. [3] incorporate multiple sources of information, including geographical data, into a deep generative framework (WaPOIR) to fuse diverse signals and address uncertainty in geographical modeling. Overall, the reviewed literature underscores the critical role of sophisticated graph-based and hierarchical models in capturing the multifaceted geographical influences on POI recommendation. These approaches range from explicit graph embedding techniques to high-order hypergraph structures and semantic correlation assessments, reflecting a comprehensive effort to model the complex spatial dependencies inherent in user mobility patterns.
